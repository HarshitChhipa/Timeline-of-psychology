<h2>Timeline of psychology </h2>
<h2><span id="Ancient_history_&ndash;_BCE" class="mw-headline">Ancient history &ndash; BCE</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>c. 1550&nbsp;BCE &ndash; The&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Ebers papyrus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ebers_papyrus">Ebers Papyrus</a>&nbsp;mentioned&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Clinical depression" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clinical_depression">depression</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Thought disorder" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thought_disorder">thought disorders</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>c. 600&nbsp;BCE &ndash; Many cities in Greece had temples to&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Asklepios" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asklepios">Asklepios</a>&nbsp;that provided cures for psychosomatic illnesses.<sup id="cite_ref-Silverberg1967_2-0" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>540&ndash;475&nbsp;<a title="Heraclitus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heraclitus">Heraclitus</a><sup id="cite_ref-SheehyChapman2002_3-0" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>c. 500&nbsp;<a title="Alcmaeon of Croton" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcmaeon_of_Croton">Alcmaeon</a><sup id="cite_ref-SheehyChapman2002_3-1" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;- suggested theory of humors as regulating human behavior (similar to&nbsp;<a title="Empedocles" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empedocles">Empedocles</a>' elements)</li>
<li>500&ndash;428&nbsp;<a title="Anaxagoras" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaxagoras">Anaxagoras</a><sup id="cite_ref-SheehyChapman2002_3-2" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>490&ndash;430&nbsp;<a title="Empedocles" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empedocles">Empedocles</a><sup id="cite_ref-SheehyChapman2002_3-3" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;proposed a first natural, non-religious system of factors that create things around, including human characters. In his model he used four elements (water, fire, earth, air) and four seasons to derive diversity of natural systems.</li>
<li>490&ndash;421&nbsp;<a title="Protagoras" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protagoras">Protagoras</a><sup id="cite_ref-SheehyChapman2002_3-4" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>470&ndash;399&nbsp;<a title="Socrates" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates">Socrates</a><sup id="cite_ref-SheehyChapman2002_3-5" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;&ndash; Socrates has been called the father of western philosophy, if only via his influence on Plato and Aristotle. Socrates made a major contribution to&nbsp;<a title="Pedagogy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedagogy">pedagogy</a>&nbsp;via his dialectical method and to&nbsp;<a title="Epistemology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistemology">epistemology</a>&nbsp;via his definition of true knowledge as true belief buttressed by some rational justification.</li>
<li>470&ndash;370&nbsp;<a title="Democritus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democritus">Democritus</a><sup id="cite_ref-SheehyChapman2002_3-6" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;&ndash; Democritus distinguished between insufficient knowledge gained through the senses and legitimate knowledge gained through the intellect&mdash;an early stance on&nbsp;<a title="Epistemology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistemology">epistemology</a>.</li>
<li>460&nbsp;BC &ndash; 370&nbsp;BCE &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Hippocrates" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a>&nbsp;introduced principles of scientific medicine based upon&nbsp;<a title="Naturalism (philosophy)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naturalism_(philosophy)">naturalistic</a>&nbsp;observation and logic, and denied the influence of spirits and demons in diseases. Introduced the concept of "temperamentum"("mixture", i.e. 4&nbsp;<a title="Temperament" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperament">temperament</a>&nbsp;types based on a ratio between chemical bodily systems.<sup id="cite_ref-HowellsOsborn1984_4-0" class="reference"></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Radden2002_5-0" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;<a title="Hippocrates" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a>&nbsp;was among the first physicians to argue that brain, and not the heart is the organ of psychic processes.</li>
<li>387&nbsp;BCE &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Plato" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato">Plato</a>&nbsp;suggested that the brain is the seat of mental processes. Plato's view of the "soul" (self) is that the body exists to serve the soul: "God created the soul before the body and gave it precedence both in time and value, and made it the dominating and controlling partner." from&nbsp;<a title="Timaeus (dialogue)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timaeus_(dialogue)">Timaeus</a><sup id="cite_ref-Kemp1990_6-0" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>c. 350&nbsp;BCE &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Aristotle" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle">Aristotle</a>&nbsp;wrote on the&nbsp;<em>psuch&ecirc;</em>&nbsp;(soul) in&nbsp;<strong><a class="mw-redirect" title="De Anima" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Anima">De Anima</a></strong>, first mentioning the&nbsp;<a title="Tabula rasa" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tabula_rasa">tabula rasa</a>&nbsp;concept of the mind.</li>
<li>c. 340&nbsp;BCE &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Praxagoras" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxagoras">Praxagoras</a></li>
<li>371&ndash;288&nbsp;<a title="Theophrastus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theophrastus">Theophrastus</a><sup id="cite_ref-SheehyChapman2002_3-7" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>341&ndash;270&nbsp;<a title="Epicurus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epicurus">Epicurus</a><sup id="cite_ref-SheehyChapman2002_3-8" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>c. 320&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Herophilus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herophilus">Herophilus</a><sup id="cite_ref-SheehyChapman2002_3-9" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>c. 300&ndash;30&nbsp;<a title="Zeno of Citium" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeno_of_Citium">Zeno of Citium</a>&nbsp;taught the philosophy of&nbsp;<a title="Stoicism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoicism">Stoicism</a>, involving&nbsp;<a title="Logic" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic">logic</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Ethics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethics">ethics</a>. In logic, he distinguished between imperfect knowledge offered by the senses and superior knowledge offered by reason. In ethics, he taught that virtue lay in reason and vice in rejection of reason. Stoicism inspired&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Aaron Beck" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aaron_Beck">Aaron Beck</a>&nbsp;to introduce&nbsp;<a title="Cognitive behavioral therapy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_behavioral_therapy">cognitive behavioral therapy</a>&nbsp;in the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>304&ndash;250&nbsp;<a title="Erasistratus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erasistratus">Erasistratus</a><sup id="cite_ref-SheehyChapman2002_3-10" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>123&ndash;43&nbsp;BCE &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Themison of Laodicea" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Themison_of_Laodicea">Themison of Laodicea</a>&nbsp;was a pupil of&nbsp;<a title="Asclepiades of Bithynia" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asclepiades_of_Bithynia">Asclepiades of Bithynia</a>&nbsp;and founded a school of medical thought known as "methodism." He was criticized by Soranus for his cruel handling of mental patients. Among his prescriptions were darkness, restraint by chains, and deprivation of food and drink.&nbsp;<a title="Juvenal" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juvenal">Juvenal</a>&nbsp;satirized him and suggested that he killed more patients than he cured.<sup id="cite_ref-HowellsOsborn1984_4-1" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>c. 100&nbsp;BCE &ndash; The&nbsp;<a title="Dead Sea Scrolls" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dead_Sea_Scrolls">Dead Sea Scrolls</a>&nbsp;noted the division of human nature into two temperaments.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="First_century" class="mw-headline">First century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>c. 50 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Aulus Cornelius Celsus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aulus_Cornelius_Celsus">Aulus Cornelius Celsus</a>&nbsp;died, leaving&nbsp;<em><a title="De Medicina" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Medicina">De Medicina</a></em>, a medical encyclopedia; Book 3 covers mental diseases. The term insania, insanity, was first used by him. The methods of treatment included bleeding, frightening the patient, emetics, enemas, total darkness, and decoctions of poppy or henbane, and pleasant ones such as&nbsp;<a title="Music therapy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music_therapy">music therapy</a>, travel, sport, reading aloud, and massage. He was aware of the importance of the doctor-patient relationship.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_9-0" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>c. 100 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Rufus of Ephesus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rufus_of_Ephesus">Rufus of Ephesus</a>&nbsp;believed that the nervous system was instrumental in voluntary movement and sensation. He discovered the optic chiasma by anatomical studies of the brain. He stressed taking a history of both physical and mental disorders. He gave a detailed account of melancholia, and was quoted by Galen.<sup id="cite_ref-HowellsOsborn1984_4-2" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>93&ndash;138 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Soranus of Ephesus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soranus_of_Ephesus">Soranus of Ephesus</a>&nbsp;advised kind treatment in healthy and comfortable conditions, including light, warm rooms.<sup id="cite_ref-HowellsOsborn1984_4-3" class="reference"></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Second_century" class="mw-headline">Second century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>c. 130&ndash;200 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Galen" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galen">Galen</a>&nbsp;"was schooled in all the psychological systems of the day: Platonic, Aristotelian, Stoic, and Epicurean"<sup id="cite_ref-Radden2002_5-1" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;He advanced medicine by offering anatomic investigations and was a skilled&nbsp;<a title="Physician" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physician">physician</a>. Galen developed further the theory of temperaments suggested by&nbsp;<a title="Hippocrates" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a>, that people's characters were determined by the balance among four bodily substances. He also distinguished sensory from motor nerves and showed that the brain controls the muscles.</li>
<li>c. 150&ndash;200 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Aretaeus of Cappadocia" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aretaeus_of_Cappadocia">Aretaeus of Cappadocia</a><sup id="cite_ref-Radden2002_5-2" class="reference"></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Third_century" class="mw-headline">Third century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>155&ndash;220&nbsp;<a title="Tertullian" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tertullian">Tertullian</a><sup id="cite_ref-SheehyChapman2002_3-11" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>205&ndash;270&nbsp;<a title="Plotinus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plotinus">Plotinus</a>&nbsp;wrote&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Enneads" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enneads">Enneads</a>&nbsp;a systematic account of Neoplatonist philosophy, also nature of visual perception and how memory might work.<sup id="cite_ref-Kemp1990_6-1" class="reference"></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Fourth_century" class="mw-headline">Fourth century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>c. 323&ndash;403 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Oribasius" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oribasius">Oribasius</a>&nbsp;compiled medical writings based on the works of&nbsp;<a title="Aristotle" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle">Aristotle</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Asclepiades of Bithynia" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asclepiades_of_Bithynia">Asclepiades</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Soranus of Ephesus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soranus_of_Ephesus">Soranus of Ephesus</a>, and wrote on melancholia in Galenic terms.<sup id="cite_ref-HowellsOsborn1984_4-4" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>345&ndash;399 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Evagrius Ponticus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evagrius_Ponticus">Evagrius Ponticus</a>&nbsp;described a rigorous way of introspection within the early Christian monastic tradition. Through introspection, monks could acquire self-knowledge and control their stream of thought which signified potentially demonic influences. Ponticus developed this view in&nbsp;<em><a title="Praktikos" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praktikos">Praktikos</a></em>, his guide to ascetic life.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>c. 390 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Nemesius" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nemesius">Nemesius</a>&nbsp;wrote&nbsp;<em><a title="Nemesius" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nemesius#De_Natura_Hominis">De Natura Hominis</a></em>&nbsp;(On Human Nature); large sections were incorporated in Saint&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="John Damascene" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Damascene">John Damascene</a>'s&nbsp;<em>De Fide Orthodoxia</em>&nbsp;in the eighth century. Nemesius' book&nbsp;<a class="new" title="De Placitis Hippocratis et Platonis (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=De_Placitis_Hippocratis_et_Platonis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">De Placitis Hippocratis et Platonis</a>&nbsp;(On the Doctrines of Hippocrates and Plato) contains many passages concerning Galen's anatomy and physiology, believing that different cavities of the brain were responsible for different functions.<sup id="cite_ref-HowellsOsborn1984_4-5" class="reference"></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kemp1990_6-2" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>397&ndash;398 &ndash;&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Augustine" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Augustine">St. Augustine of Hippo</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="mw-redirect" title="Confessions (St. Augustine)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confessions_(St._Augustine)">Confessions</a></em>, which anticipated Freud by near-discovery of the subconscious.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;Augustine's most complete account of the soul is in&nbsp;<a class="new" title="De Quantitate Animae (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=De_Quantitate_Animae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">De Quantitate Animae</a>&nbsp;(The Greatness of the Soul). The work assumes a Platonic model of the soul.<sup id="cite_ref-Kemp1990_6-3" class="reference"></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Fifth_century" class="mw-headline">Fifth century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>5th century &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Caelius Aurelianus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caelius_Aurelianus">Caelius Aurelianus</a>&nbsp;opposed harsh methods of handling the insane, and advocated humane treatment.<sup id="cite_ref-HowellsOsborn1984_4-6" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>c. 423&ndash;529 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Theodosius the Cenobiarch" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodosius_the_Cenobiarch">Theodosius the Cenobiarch</a>&nbsp;founded a monastery at Kathismus, near Bethlehem. Three hospitals were built by the side of the monastery: one for the sick, one for the aged, and one for the insane.<sup id="cite_ref-HowellsOsborn1984_4-7" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>c. 451 &ndash; Patriarch&nbsp;<a title="Nestorius" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nestorius">Nestorius</a>&nbsp;of Constantinople: his followers dedicated themselves to the sick and became physicians of great repute. They brought the works of&nbsp;<a title="Hippocrates" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Aristotle" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Galen" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galen">Galen</a>, and influenced the approach to physical and mental disorders in Persia and Arabia<sup id="cite_ref-HowellsOsborn1984_4-8" class="reference"></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Seventh_century" class="mw-headline">Seventh century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>625&ndash;690 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Paul of Aegina" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_of_Aegina">Paul of Aegina</a>&nbsp;suggested that hysteria should be treated by ligature of the limbs, and mania by tying the patient to a mattress placed inside a wicker basket and suspended from the ceiling. He also recommended baths, wine, special diets, and sedatives for the mentally ill. He described the following mental disorders: phrenitis, delirium, lethargus, melancholia, mania, incubus, lycanthropy, and epilepsy</li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Ninth_century" class="mw-headline">Ninth century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>c. 800 &ndash; The first&nbsp;<a title="Bimaristan" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bimaristan">bimaristan</a>&nbsp;was built in&nbsp;<a title="Baghdad" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baghdad">Baghdad</a>. By the 13th century, bimaristans grew into hospitals with specialized wards, including wards for mentally ill patients.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>c. 850 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Ali ibn Sahl Rabban al-Tabari" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ali_ibn_Sahl_Rabban_al-Tabari">Ali ibn Sahl Rabban al-Tabari</a>&nbsp;wrote a work emphasizing the need for&nbsp;<a title="Psychotherapy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychotherapy">psychotherapy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Tenth_century" class="mw-headline">Tenth century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>c. 900 &ndash;&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Ahmed ibn Sahl al-Balkhi" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmed_ibn_Sahl_al-Balkhi">Ahmed ibn Sahl al-Balkhi</a>&nbsp;urged doctors to ensure that they evaluated the state of both their patients' bodies and&nbsp;<a title="Soul" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soul">souls</a>, and highlighted the link between spiritual or mental health and overall health.<sup id="cite_ref-Talib_14-0" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>c. 900 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Al-Razi" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Razi">al-Razi</a>&nbsp;(Rhazes) promoted psychotherapy and an understanding attitude towards those suffering from psychological distress.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Eleventh_century" class="mw-headline">Eleventh century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>1025 &ndash; In&nbsp;<em><a title="The Canon of Medicine" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Canon_of_Medicine">The Canon of Medicine</a></em>,&nbsp;<a title="Avicenna" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avicenna">Avicenna</a>&nbsp;described a number of conditions, including&nbsp;<a title="Hallucination" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hallucination">hallucination</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Insomnia" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insomnia">insomnia</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Mania" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mania">mania</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Nightmare" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nightmare">nightmare</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Melancholia" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melancholia">melancholia</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Dementia" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dementia">dementia</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Epilepsy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epilepsy">epilepsy</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Paralysis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paralysis">paralysis</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Stroke" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stroke">stroke</a>,&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Vertigo (medical)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertigo_(medical)">vertigo</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Tremor" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tremor">tremor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>c. 1030 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Al-Biruni" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Biruni">Al-Biruni</a>&nbsp;employed an&nbsp;<a title="Experiment" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experiment">experimental</a>&nbsp;method in examining the concept of&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Reaction time" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_time">reaction time</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Twelfth_century" class="mw-headline">Twelfth century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>c. 1200 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Maimonides" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maimonides">Maimonides</a>&nbsp;wrote about&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Neuropsychiatric" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuropsychiatric">neuropsychiatric</a>&nbsp;disorders, and described&nbsp;<a title="Rabies" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rabies">rabies</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Deadly nightshade" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deadly_nightshade">belladonna</a>&nbsp;intoxication.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Thirteenth_century" class="mw-headline">Thirteenth century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>c. 1180 &ndash; 1245&nbsp;<a title="Alexander of Hales" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_of_Hales">Alexander of Hales</a></li>
<li>c. 1190 &ndash; 1249&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="William of Auvergne (bishop)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_of_Auvergne_(bishop)">William of Auvergne</a></li>
<li>1215&ndash;1277&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Peter Juliani" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Juliani">Peter Juliani</a>&nbsp;taught in the medical faculty of the University of Siena, and wrote on medical, philosophical and psychological topics. He was personal physician to&nbsp;<a title="Pope Gregory X" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_X">Pope Gregory X</a>&nbsp;and later became archbishop and cardinal. He was elected pope under the name&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="John XXI" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_XXI">John XXI</a>&nbsp;in 1276.<sup id="cite_ref-Kemp1990_6-4" class="reference"></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CosmanJones2009_18-0" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>c. 1214 &ndash; 1294&nbsp;<a title="Roger Bacon" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Bacon">Roger Bacon</a>&nbsp;advocated for empirical methods and wrote on optics, visual perception, and linguistics.</li>
<li>1221&ndash;1274&nbsp;<a title="Bonaventure" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonaventure">Bonaventure</a></li>
<li>1193&ndash;1280&nbsp;<a title="Albertus Magnus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albertus_Magnus">Albertus Magnus</a></li>
<li>1225 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Thomas Aquinas" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a></li>
<li>1240 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Bartholomeus Anglicus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartholomeus_Anglicus">Bartholomeus Anglicus</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em>De Proprietatibus Rerum</em>, which included a dissertation on the brain, recognizing that mental disorders can have a physical or psychological cause.</li>
<li>1247 &ndash;&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Bethlehem Royal Hospital" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bethlehem_Royal_Hospital">Bethlehem Royal Hospital</a>&nbsp;in Bishopsgate outside the wall of London, one of the most famous old psychiatric hospitals was founded as a priory of the Order of St. Mary of Bethlem to collect alms for Crusaders; after the English government secularized it, it started admitting mental patients by 1377 (c. 1403), becoming known as Bedlam Hospital; in 1547 it was acquired by the City of London, operating until 1948; it is now part of the British&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="NHS Foundation Trust" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NHS_Foundation_Trust">NHS Foundation Trust</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>1266&ndash;1308&nbsp;<a title="Duns Scotus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duns_Scotus">Duns Scotus</a></li>
<li>c. 1270 &ndash;&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Witelo" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Witelo">Witelo</a>&nbsp;wrote&nbsp;<em><a class="mw-redirect" title="Perspectiva" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspectiva">Perspectiva</a></em>, a work on optics containing speculations on psychology, nearly discovering the subconscious.</li>
<li>1295&nbsp;<a title="Lanfranc" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lanfranc">Lanfranc</a>&nbsp;writes&nbsp;<em>Science of Cirurgie</em><sup id="cite_ref-Kemp1990_6-5" class="reference"></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Fourteenth_century" class="mw-headline">Fourteenth century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>1317&ndash;40 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="William of Ockham" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_of_Ockham">William of Ockham</a>, an English Franciscan friar and scholastic philosopher and theologian, is commonly known for&nbsp;<a title="Occam's razor" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occam%27s_razor">Occam's razor</a>, the methodological principle that the simplest explanation is to be preferred. He also produced significant works on logic, physics, and theology, advancing his thoughts about intuitive and abstracted knowledge.</li>
<li>1347&ndash;50 &ndash; The&nbsp;<a title="Black Death" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Death">Black Death</a>&nbsp;devastated Europe.</li>
<li>c. 1375 &ndash; English authorities regarded mental illness as demonic possession, treating it with&nbsp;<a title="Exorcism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exorcism">exorcism</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Torture" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torture">torture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-manoneileen_20-0" class="reference"></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Fifteenth_century" class="mw-headline">Fifteenth century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>c. 1400 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Renaissance humanism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renaissance_humanism">Renaissance Humanism</a>&nbsp;caused a reawakening of ancient knowledge of science and medicine.</li>
<li>1433&ndash;1499&nbsp;<a title="Marsilio Ficino" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marsilio_Ficino">Marsilio Ficino</a>&nbsp;was a renowned figure of the Italian Renaissance, a Neoplatonist humanist, a translator of Greek philosophical writing, and the most influential exponent of Platonism in Italy in the fifteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-Radden2002_5-3" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>c. 1450 &ndash; The pendulum in Europe swings, bringing&nbsp;<a title="European witchcraft" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_witchcraft">witch mania</a>, causing thousands of women to be executed for witchcraft until the late 17th century.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Sixteenth_century" class="mw-headline">Sixteenth century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>1590 &ndash; Scholastic philosopher&nbsp;<a title="Rudolph Goclenius" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolph_Goclenius">Rudolph Goclenius</a>&nbsp;coined the term "psychology"; though usually regarded as the origin of the term, there is evidence that it was used at least six decades earlier by&nbsp;<a title="Marko Marulić" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marko_Maruli%C4%87">Marko Marulić</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Seventeenth_century" class="mw-headline">Seventeenth century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>c. 1600&ndash;1625 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Francis Bacon" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis_Bacon">Francis Bacon</a>&nbsp;was an English philosopher, statesman, scientist, lawyer, jurist, author, and pioneer of the scientific method. His writings on psychological topics included the nature of knowledge and memory.</li>
<li>1650 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Ren&eacute; Descartes" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes">Ren&eacute; Descartes</a>&nbsp;died, leaving&nbsp;<em><a title="The World (Descartes)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_World_(Descartes)">Treatise of the World</a></em>, containing his dualistic theory of reality, mind vs. matter.</li>
<li>1672 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Thomas Willis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Willis">Thomas Willis</a>&nbsp;published the anatomical treatise&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="De Anima Brutorum (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=De_Anima_Brutorum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">De Anima Brutorum</a></em>, describing psychology in terms of brain function.</li>
<li>1677 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Baruch Spinoza" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baruch_Spinoza">Baruch Spinoza</a>&nbsp;died, leaving&nbsp;<a title="Ethics (Spinoza)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethics_(Spinoza)">Ethics, Demonstrated in Geometrical Order</a>, Pt. 2 focusing on the human mind and body, disputing Descartes and arguing that they are one, and Pt. 3 attempting to show that moral concepts such as good and evil, virtue, and perfection have a basis in human psychology.</li>
<li>1689 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="John Locke" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Locke">John Locke</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a title="An Essay Concerning Human Understanding" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/An_Essay_Concerning_Human_Understanding">An Essay Concerning Human Understanding</a></em>, which claims that the human mind is a Tabula Rasa at birth.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Eighteenth_century" class="mw-headline">Eighteenth century</span></h2>
<ul>
<li>1701 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz">Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz</a>&nbsp;published the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Law of Continuity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_Continuity">Law of Continuity</a>, which he applied to psychology, becoming the first to postulate an unconscious mind; he also introduced the concept of threshold.<sup id="cite_ref-google_21-0" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>1710 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="George Berkeley" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Berkeley">George Berkeley</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="mw-redirect" title="Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treatise_Concerning_the_Principles_of_Human_Knowledge">Treatise Concerning the Principles of Human Knowledge</a></em>, which claims that the outside world is composed solely of ideas.</li>
<li>1732 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Christian Wolff (philosopher)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Wolff_(philosopher)">Christian Wolff</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="Psychologia Empirica (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Psychologia_Empirica&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">Psychologia Empirica</a></em>, followed in 1734 by&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="Psychologia Rationalis (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Psychologia_Rationalis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">Psychologia Rationalis</a></em>, popularizing the term "psychology".</li>
<li>1739 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="David Hume" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hume">David Hume</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a title="A Treatise of Human Nature" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Treatise_of_Human_Nature">A Treatise of Human Nature</a></em>, claiming that all contents of mind are solely built from sense experiences.</li>
<li>1781 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Immanuel Kant" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immanuel_Kant">Immanuel Kant</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a title="Critique of Pure Reason" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critique_of_Pure_Reason">Critique of Pure Reason</a></em>, rejecting Hume's extreme empiricism and proposing that there is more to knowledge than bare sense experience, distinguishing between "a posteriori" and "a priori" knowledge, the former being derived from perception, hence occurring after perception, and the latter being a property of thought, independent of experience and existing before experience.</li>
<li>1783 &ndash;&nbsp;<a class="new" title="Ferdinand Ueberwasser (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ferdinand_Ueberwasser&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">Ferdinand Ueberwasser</a>&nbsp;designated himself&nbsp;<em>Professor of Empirical Psychology and Logic</em>&nbsp;at the Old University of M&uuml;nster; four years later, he published the comprehensive textbook&nbsp;<em>Instructions for the regular study of empirical psychology for candidates of philosophy at the University of M&uuml;nster</em>&nbsp;which complemented his lectures on scientific psychology.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>1798 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Immanuel Kant" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immanuel_Kant">Immanuel Kant</a>&nbsp;proposed the first dimensional model of consistent individual differences by mapping the four&nbsp;<a title="Hippocrates" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a>'&nbsp;<a title="Temperament" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperament">temperament</a>&nbsp;types into dimensions of emotionality and energetic arousal.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;These two dimensions later became an essential part of all temperament and personality models.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Nineteenth_century" class="mw-headline">Nineteenth century</span></h2>
<h3><span id="1800s" class="mw-headline">1800s</span></h3>
<ul>
<li>c. 1800 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Franz Joseph Gall" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franz_Joseph_Gall">Franz Joseph Gall</a>&nbsp;developed cranioscopy, the measurement of the skull to determine psychological characteristics, which was later renamed&nbsp;<a title="Phrenology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrenology">phrenology</a>; it is now discredited.</li>
<li>1807 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel">Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="mw-redirect" title="Phenomenology of Spirit" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenomenology_of_Spirit">Phenomenology of Spirit</a></em>&nbsp;(Mind), which describes his thesis-antithesis-synthesis dialectical method, according to which knowledge pushes forwards to greater certainty, and ultimately towards knowledge of the noumenal world.</li>
<li>1808 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Johann Christian Reil" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Christian_Reil">Johann Christian Reil</a>&nbsp;coined the term "psychiatry".</li>
</ul>
<h3><span id="1810s" class="mw-headline">1810s</span></h3>
<ul>
<li>1812 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Benjamin Rush" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Rush">Benjamin Rush</a>&nbsp;became one of the earliest advocates of humane treatment for the mentally ill with the publication of&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="Medical Inquiries and Observations Upon Diseases of the Mind (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Medical_Inquiries_and_Observations_Upon_Diseases_of_the_Mind&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">Medical Inquiries and Observations Upon Diseases of the Mind</a></em>,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;the first American textbook on psychiatry.<sup id="cite_ref-MentalWellnesscom_25-0" class="reference"></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span id="1820s" class="mw-headline">1820s</span></h3>
<ul>
<li>1829 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="John Stuart Mill" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill">John Stuart Mill</a>'s father&nbsp;<a title="James Mill" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Mill">James Mill</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="Analysis of the Phenomena of the Human Mind (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Analysis_of_the_Phenomena_of_the_Human_Mind&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">Analysis of the Phenomena of the Human Mind</a></em>&nbsp;(2 vols.).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span id="1840s" class="mw-headline">1840s</span></h3>
<ul>
<li>1840 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Frederick Augustus Rauch" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_Augustus_Rauch">Frederick Augustus Rauch</a>&nbsp;(1806&ndash;1841) published&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="Psychology, or a View of the Human Soul, including Anthropology (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Psychology,_or_a_View_of_the_Human_Soul,_including_Anthropology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">Psychology, or a View of the Human Soul, including Anthropology</a></em></li>
<li>1843 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Forbes Benignus Winslow" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forbes_Benignus_Winslow">Forbes Benignus Winslow</a>&nbsp;(1810&ndash;1874) published&nbsp;<em>The Plea of Insanity in Criminal Cases</em>, helping establish the plea of insanity in criminal cases in Britain.</li>
<li>1844 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="S&oslash;ren Kierkegaard" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%B8ren_Kierkegaard">S&oslash;ren Kierkegaard</a>&nbsp;<em><a title="The Concept of Anxiety" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Concept_of_Anxiety">The Concept of Anxiety</a></em>, the first exposition on&nbsp;<a title="Anxiety" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anxiety">anxiety</a>.</li>
<li>1848 &ndash; Vermont railroad worker&nbsp;<a title="Phineas Gage" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phineas_Gage">Phineas Gage</a>&nbsp;had a 3-foot rod driven through his brain and jaw in an explosives accident, permanently changing his personality, revolutionizing scientific opinion about brain functions being localizable.</li>
<li>1849 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="S&oslash;ren Kierkegaard" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%B8ren_Kierkegaard">S&oslash;ren Kierkegaard</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a title="The Sickness Unto Death" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Sickness_Unto_Death">The Sickness Unto Death</a></em>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span id="1850s" class="mw-headline">1850s</span></h3>
<ul>
<li>1852 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Hermann Lotze" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Lotze">Hermann Lotze</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="Medical Psychology or Physiology of the Soul (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Medical_Psychology_or_Physiology_of_the_Soul&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">Medical Psychology or Physiology of the Soul</a></em>.</li>
<li>1856 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Hermann Lotze" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Lotze">Hermann Lotze</a>&nbsp;began publishing his 3-volume magnum opus&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="Mikrokosmos (Hermann Lotze) (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mikrokosmos_(Hermann_Lotze)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">Mikrokosmos</a></em>&nbsp;(1856&ndash;64), arguing that natural laws of inanimate objects apply to human minds and bodies but have the function of enabling us to aim for the values set by the deity, thus making room for aesthetics.</li>
<li>1859 &ndash;&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Pierre Briquet" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Briquet">Pierre Briquet</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="Traite Clinique et Therapeutique de L'Hysterie (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Traite_Clinique_et_Therapeutique_de_L%27Hysterie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">Traite Clinique et Therapeutique de L'Hysterie</a></em>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span id="1860s" class="mw-headline">1860s</span></h3>
<ul>
<li>1860s &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Franciscus Donders" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franciscus_Donders">Franciscus Donders</a>&nbsp;first used human reaction time to infer differences in cognitive processing.</li>
<li>1860 &ndash;&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Gustav Theodor Fechner" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Theodor_Fechner">Gustav Theodor Fechner</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="Elements of Psychophysics (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Elements_of_Psychophysics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">Elements of Psychophysics</a></em>, founding the subject of&nbsp;<a title="Psychophysics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychophysics">psychophysics</a>.</li>
<li>1861 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Paul Broca" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Broca">Paul Broca</a>&nbsp;discovered an area in the left&nbsp;<a title="Cerebral hemisphere" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cerebral_hemisphere">cerebral hemisphere</a>&nbsp;that is important for speech production, now known as&nbsp;<a title="Broca's area" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Broca%27s_area">Broca's area</a>, founding&nbsp;<a title="Neuropsychology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuropsychology">neuropsychology</a>.</li>
<li>1869 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Francis Galton" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis_Galton">Francis Galton</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a title="Greatness" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greatness#Hereditary_Genius_.E2.80.93_Galton_.281869.29">Hereditary Genius</a></em>, arguing for&nbsp;<a title="Eugenics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugenics">eugenics</a>. He went on to found&nbsp;<a title="Psychometrics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychometrics">psychometrics</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Differential psychology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_psychology">differential psychology</a>, and the&nbsp;<a title="Lexical hypothesis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lexical_hypothesis">lexical hypothesis</a>&nbsp;of personality.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span id="1870s" class="mw-headline">1870s</span></h3>
<ul>
<li>1872 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Douglas Spalding" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_Spalding">Douglas Spalding</a>&nbsp;published his discovery of&nbsp;<a title="Imprinting (psychology)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imprinting_(psychology)">psychological imprinting</a>.</li>
<li>1874 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Wilhelm Wundt" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Wundt">Wilhelm Wundt</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="mw-redirect" title="Grundz&uuml;ge der physiologischen Psychologie" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grundz%C3%BCge_der_physiologischen_Psychologie">Grundz&uuml;ge der physiologischen Psychologie</a></em>&nbsp;(Principles of Physiological Psychology), the first textbook of experimental psychology.</li>
<li>1878 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="G. Stanley Hall" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G._Stanley_Hall">G. Stanley Hall</a>&nbsp;was awarded the first PhD on a psychological topic from Harvard (in philosophy).</li>
<li>1879 &ndash; Wilhelm Wundt opened the first experimental psychology laboratory at the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="University of Leipzig" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Leipzig">University of Leipzig</a>&nbsp;in Germany.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span id="1880s" class="mw-headline">1880s</span></h3>
<ul>
<li>1882 &ndash; The&nbsp;<a title="Society for Psychical Research" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Society_for_Psychical_Research">Society for Psychical Research</a>&nbsp;was founded in England.</li>
<li>1883 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="G. Stanley Hall" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G._Stanley_Hall">G. Stanley Hall</a>&nbsp;opened the first American experimental psychology research laboratory at&nbsp;<a title="Johns Hopkins University" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University">Johns Hopkins University</a>.</li>
<li>1883 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Emil Kraepelin" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emil_Kraepelin">Emil Kraepelin</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="Compendium der Psychiatrie (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Compendium_der_Psychiatrie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">Compendium der Psychiatrie</a></em>.</li>
<li>1884 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Ivan Pavlov" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ivan_Pavlov">Ivan Pavlov</a>&nbsp;began studying the digestive secretion of animals.</li>
<li>1884 &ndash;&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Tourette's syndrome" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tourette%27s_syndrome">Tourette's Syndrome</a>&nbsp;was first described.</li>
<li>1885 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Hermann Ebbinghaus" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Ebbinghaus">Hermann Ebbinghaus</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="&Uuml;ber das Ged&auml;chtnis (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=%C3%9Cber_das_Ged%C3%A4chtnis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">&Uuml;ber das Ged&auml;chtnis</a></em>&nbsp;(On Memory), a groundbreaking work based on self-experiments, first describing the learning curve, forgetting curve, and spacing effect.</li>
<li>1886 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="John Dewey" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Dewey">John Dewey</a>&nbsp;published the first American textbook on psychology, titled&nbsp;<em><a title="Psychology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychology">Psychology</a></em>.</li>
<li>1886 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Vladimir Bekhterev" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Bekhterev">Vladimir Bekhterev</a>&nbsp;established the first laboratory of experimental psychology in Russia at&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Kazan University" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazan_University">Kazan University</a>.</li>
<li>1886 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Sigmund Freud" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigmund_Freud">Sigmund Freud</a>&nbsp;began private practice in&nbsp;<a title="Vienna" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vienna">Vienna</a>.</li>
<li>1887 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Georg Elias M&uuml;ller" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Elias_M%C3%BCller">Georg Elias M&uuml;ller</a>&nbsp;opened the 2nd German experimental psychology research laboratory in&nbsp;<a title="G&ouml;ttingen" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen">G&ouml;ttingen</a>.</li>
<li>1887 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="George Trumbull Ladd" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Trumbull_Ladd">George Trumbull Ladd</a>&nbsp;(<a class="mw-redirect" title="Yale" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yale">Yale</a>) published&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="Elements of Physiological Psychology (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Elements_of_Physiological_Psychology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">Elements of Physiological Psychology</a></em>, the first American textbook to include a substantial amount of information on the new experimental form of the discipline.</li>
<li>1887 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="James McKeen Cattell" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_McKeen_Cattell">James McKeen Cattell</a>&nbsp;founded an experimental psychology laboratory at the&nbsp;<a title="University of Pennsylvania" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>, the 3rd in the United States.</li>
<li>1887 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="G. Stanley Hall" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G._Stanley_Hall">G. Stanley Hall</a>&nbsp;founded the&nbsp;<em><a title="American Journal of Psychology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Journal_of_Psychology">American Journal of Psychology</a></em>&nbsp;with a $500 contribution supplied by&nbsp;<a title="Robert Pearsall Smith" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Pearsall_Smith">Robert Pearsall Smith</a>&nbsp;of the&nbsp;<a title="American Society for Psychical Research" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Society_for_Psychical_Research">American Society for Psychical Research</a>.</li>
<li>1888 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="William Lowe Bryan" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Lowe_Bryan">William Lowe Bryan</a>&nbsp;founded the United States' 4th experimental psychology laboratory at&nbsp;<a title="Indiana University Bloomington" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indiana_University_Bloomington">Indiana University</a>.</li>
<li>1888 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Joseph Jastrow" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Jastrow">Joseph Jastrow</a>&nbsp;founded the United States' 5th experimental psychology laboratory at the&nbsp;<a title="University of Wisconsin&ndash;Madison" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison">University of Wisconsin&ndash;Madison</a>.</li>
<li>1888 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="G. Stanley Hall" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G._Stanley_Hall">G. Stanley Hall</a>&nbsp;left&nbsp;<a title="Johns Hopkins University" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University">Johns Hopkins</a>&nbsp;for the presidency of the newly founded&nbsp;<a title="Clark University" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clark_University">Clark University</a>&nbsp;in Worcester, Mass.</li>
<li>1889 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="James Mark Baldwin" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Mark_Baldwin">James Mark Baldwin</a>&nbsp;published the first volume of his&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="Handbook of Psychology (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Handbook_of_Psychology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">Handbook of Psychology</a></em>, titled "Sense and Intellect".</li>
<li>1889 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Edmund Sanford" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmund_Sanford">Edmund Sanford</a>, a former student of&nbsp;<a title="G. Stanley Hall" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G._Stanley_Hall">G. Stanley Hall</a>&nbsp;founded the United States' 6th experimental psychology laboratory at Clark University.</li>
<li>1889 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Edward Cowles" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Cowles">Edward Cowles</a>&nbsp;founded the United States' 7th experimental psychology laboratory at the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="McLean Asylum" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/McLean_Asylum">McLean Asylum</a>&nbsp;in Waverley, Mass.</li>
<li>1889 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Harry Kirke Wolfe" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_Kirke_Wolfe">Harry Kirke Wolfe</a>&nbsp;founded the United States' 8th experimental psychology laboratory at the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="University of Nebraska" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Nebraska">University of Nebraska</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span id="1890s" class="mw-headline">1890s</span></h3>
<ul>
<li>1890 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Christian von Ehrenfels" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_von_Ehrenfels">Christian von Ehrenfels</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="On the Qualities of Form (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=On_the_Qualities_of_Form&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">On the Qualities of Form</a></em>, founding&nbsp;<a title="Gestalt psychology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gestalt_psychology">Gestalt psychology</a>.</li>
<li>1890 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="William James" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_James">William James</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a title="The Principles of Psychology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Principles_of_Psychology">The Principles of Psychology</a></em>.</li>
<li>1890 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="James Hayden Tufts" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Hayden_Tufts">James Hayden Tufts</a>&nbsp;founded the United States' 9th experimental psychology laboratory at the&nbsp;<a title="University of Michigan" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Michigan">University of Michigan</a>.</li>
<li>1890 &ndash;&nbsp;<a class="new" title="G. T. W. Patrick (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=G._T._W._Patrick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">G. T. W. Patrick</a>&nbsp;founded the United States' 10th experimental psychology laboratory at the&nbsp;<a title="University of Iowa" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Iowa">University of Iowa</a>.</li>
<li>1890 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="James McKeen Cattell" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_McKeen_Cattell">James McKeen Cattell</a>&nbsp;left Pennsylvania for&nbsp;<a title="Columbia University" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbia_University">Columbia University</a>&nbsp;where he founded the United States' 11th experimental psychology laboratory.</li>
<li>1890 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="James Mark Baldwin" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Mark_Baldwin">James Mark Baldwin</a>&nbsp;founded the first permanent experimental psychology laboratory in the British Empire at the&nbsp;<a title="University of Toronto" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Toronto">University of Toronto</a>.</li>
<li>1891 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Frank Angell" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Angell">Frank Angell</a>&nbsp;founded the United States' 12th experimental psychology laboratory at the&nbsp;<a title="Cornell University" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornell_University">Cornell University</a>.</li>
<li>1891 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Edvard Westermarck" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edvard_Westermarck">Edvard Westermarck</a>&nbsp;described the&nbsp;<a title="Westermarck effect" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westermarck_effect">Westermarck effect</a>, where people raised early in life in close domestic proximity later become desensitized to close sexual attraction, raising theories about the incest taboo.</li>
<li>1892 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="G. Stanley Hall" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G._Stanley_Hall">G. Stanley Hall</a>&nbsp;et al. founded the&nbsp;<a title="American Psychological Association" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Psychological_Association">American Psychological Association</a>&nbsp;(APA).</li>
<li>1892 &ndash;&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Edward Bradford Titchener" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Bradford_Titchener">Edward Bradford Titchener</a>&nbsp;took a professorship at&nbsp;<a title="Cornell University" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornell_University">Cornell University</a>, replacing&nbsp;<a title="Frank Angell" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Angell">Frank Angell</a>&nbsp;who left for&nbsp;<a title="Stanford University" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford_University">Stanford University</a>.</li>
<li>1892 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Edward Wheeler Scripture" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Wheeler_Scripture">Edward Wheeler Scripture</a>&nbsp;founded the experimental psychology laboratory at&nbsp;<a title="Yale University" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yale_University">Yale University</a>, the 19th in United States.</li>
<li>1892&ndash;1893 &ndash;&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Charles A. Strong" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_A._Strong">Charles A. Strong</a>&nbsp;opened the experimental psychology laboratory at the&nbsp;<a title="University of Chicago" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, the 20th in the United States, at which&nbsp;<a title="James Rowland Angell" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Rowland_Angell">James Rowland Angell</a>&nbsp;conducted the first experiments of&nbsp;<a title="Functional psychology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_psychology">functionalism</a>&nbsp;in 1896.</li>
<li>1894 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Margaret Floy Washburn" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Floy_Washburn">Margaret Floy Washburn</a>&nbsp;was the first woman to be granted a PhD in Psychology after she studied under&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="E. B. Titchener" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E._B._Titchener">E. B. Titchener</a>&nbsp;at&nbsp;<a title="Cornell University" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornell_University">Cornell University</a>.</li>
<li>1894 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="James McKeen Cattell" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_McKeen_Cattell">James McKeen Cattell</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="James Mark Baldwin" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Mark_Baldwin">James Mark Baldwin</a>&nbsp;founded the&nbsp;<em><a title="Psychological Review" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychological_Review">Psychological Review</a></em>&nbsp;to compete with&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Granville Stanley Hall" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Granville_Stanley_Hall">Hall</a>'s&nbsp;<em><a title="American Journal of Psychology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Journal_of_Psychology">American Journal of Psychology</a></em>.</li>
<li>1895 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Gustave Le Bon" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustave_Le_Bon">Gustave Le Bon</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a title="The Crowd: A Study of the Popular Mind" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Crowd:_A_Study_of_the_Popular_Mind">The Crowd: A Study of the Popular Mind</a></em>.</li>
<li>1896 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="John Dewey" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Dewey">John Dewey</a>&nbsp;published the paper&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="The Reflex Arc Concept in Psychology (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Reflex_Arc_Concept_in_Psychology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">The Reflex Arc Concept in Psychology</a></em>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"></sup>&nbsp;founding&nbsp;<a title="Functionalism (philosophy of mind)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functionalism_(philosophy_of_mind)">functionalism</a>.</li>
<li>1896 &ndash; The first psychological clinic was opened at the&nbsp;<a title="University of Pennsylvania" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>&nbsp;by&nbsp;<a title="Lightner Witmer" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightner_Witmer">Lightner Witmer</a>; although often celebrated as marking the birth of&nbsp;<a title="Clinical psychology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clinical_psychology">clinical psychology</a>, it was focused primarily on educational matters.</li>
<li>1896 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Edward B. Titchener" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_B._Titchener">Edward B. Titchener</a>, student of&nbsp;<a title="Wilhelm Wundt" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Wundt">Wilhelm Wundt</a>&nbsp;and originator of the terms "<a title="Structuralism (psychology)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structuralism_(psychology)">structuralism</a>" and "<a title="Functional psychology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_psychology">functionalism</a>" published&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="An Outline of Psychology (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=An_Outline_of_Psychology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">An Outline of Psychology</a></em>.</li>
<li>1897 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Havelock Ellis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Havelock_Ellis">Havelock Ellis</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="Sexual Inversion (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sexual_Inversion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">Sexual Inversion</a></em>.</li>
<li>1898 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Boris Sidis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boris_Sidis">Boris Sidis</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="The Psychology of Suggestion: A Research into the Subconscious Nature of Man and Society (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Psychology_of_Suggestion:_A_Research_into_the_Subconscious_Nature_of_Man_and_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">The Psychology of Suggestion: A Research into the Subconscious Nature of Man and Society</a></em>.</li>
<li>1899 &ndash; On 4 November&nbsp;<a title="Sigmund Freud" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigmund_Freud">Sigmund Freud</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a title="The Interpretation of Dreams" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Interpretation_of_Dreams">The Interpretation of Dreams</a></em>&nbsp;(Die Traumdeutung), marking the beginning of&nbsp;<a title="Psychoanalysis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychoanalysis">psychoanalysis</a>, which attempts to deal with the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Oedipal complex" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oedipal_complex">Oedipal complex</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span id="Twentieth_century" class="mw-headline">Twentieth century</span></h2>
<h3><span id="1900s" class="mw-headline">1900s</span></h3>
<ul>
<li>1901 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Sigmund Freud" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigmund_Freud">Sigmund Freud</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a title="The Psychopathology of Everyday Life" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Psychopathology_of_Everyday_Life">The Psychopathology of Everyday Life</a></em>.</li>
<li>1903 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="John B. Watson" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_B._Watson">John B. Watson</a>&nbsp;graduated from the University of Chicago; his dissertation on rat behavior has been described as a "classic of developmental psychobiology" by historian of psychology&nbsp;<a title="Donald Dewsbury" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Dewsbury">Donald Dewsbury</a>.</li>
<li>1903 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Helen Thompson Woolley" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helen_Thompson_Woolley">Helen Thompson Woolley</a>&nbsp;published her doctoral dissertation,&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="The Mental Traits of Sex (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Mental_Traits_of_Sex&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">The Mental Traits of Sex</a></em>, for which she had conducted the first experimental test of sex differences.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>1904 -&nbsp;<a title="Ivan Pavlov" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ivan_Pavlov">Ivan Pavlov</a>&nbsp;won the Nobel Prize for his studies of conditioning. This was the first Prize given for research adopted by psychologists.</li>
<li>1904 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Charles Spearman" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Spearman">Charles Spearman</a>&nbsp;published the article&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="General Intelligence (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=General_Intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">General Intelligence</a></em>&nbsp;in the&nbsp;<em><a title="American Journal of Psychology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Journal_of_Psychology">American Journal of Psychology</a></em>, introducing the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="General intelligence factor" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_intelligence_factor"><em>g</em>&nbsp;factor</a>&nbsp;theory of&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Intelligence (trait)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intelligence_(trait)">intelligence</a>.</li>
<li>1905 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Alfred Binet" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Binet">Alfred Binet</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Theodore Simon" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodore_Simon">Theodore Simon</a>&nbsp;created the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Binet-Simon scale" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binet-Simon_scale">Binet-Simon scale</a>&nbsp;to identify students needing extra help, marking the beginning of standardized&nbsp;<a title="Psychological testing" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychological_testing">psychological testing</a>.</li>
<li>1905 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Edward Thorndike" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Thorndike">Edward Thorndike</a>&nbsp;published the&nbsp;<a title="Law of effect" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_effect">law of effect</a>.</li>
<li>1905 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Sigmund Freud" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigmund_Freud">Sigmund Freud</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a title="Three Essays on the Theory of Sexuality" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Essays_on_the_Theory_of_Sexuality">Three Essays on the Theory of Sexuality</a></em>.</li>
<li>1906 &ndash; The&nbsp;<a title="Journal of Abnormal Psychology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journal_of_Abnormal_Psychology">Journal of Abnormal Psychology</a>&nbsp;was founded by&nbsp;<a title="Morton Prince" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morton_Prince">Morton Prince</a>, for which&nbsp;<a title="Boris Sidis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boris_Sidis">Boris Sidis</a>&nbsp;was an associate editor and significant contributor.</li>
<li>1908 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Sigmund Freud" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigmund_Freud">Sigmund Freud</a>&nbsp;published the paper&nbsp;<em>On the Sexual Theories of Children</em>, introducing the concept of&nbsp;<a title="Penis envy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penis_envy">penis envy</a>; he also published the paper '<em>Civilized' Sexual Morality and Modern Nervous Illness</em>.</li>
<li>1908 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Wilfred Trotter" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilfred_Trotter">Wilfred Trotter</a>&nbsp;published the first paper explaining the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Herd instinct" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herd_instinct">herd instinct</a>.</li>
<li>1909 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Sigmund Freud" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigmund_Freud">Sigmund Freud</a>&nbsp;lectured at&nbsp;<a title="Clark University" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clark_University">Clark University</a>, winning over the U.S. establishment.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span id="1910s" class="mw-headline">1910s</span></h3>
<ul>
<li>1910 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Sigmund Freud" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigmund_Freud">Sigmund Freud</a>&nbsp;founded the&nbsp;<a title="International Psychoanalytical Association" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Psychoanalytical_Association">International Psychoanalytical Association</a>&nbsp;(IPA), with&nbsp;<a title="Carl Jung" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Jung">Carl Jung</a>&nbsp;as the first president, and&nbsp;<a title="Otto Rank" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_Rank">Otto Rank</a>&nbsp;as the first secretary.</li>
<li>1910 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Grace Helen Kent" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grace_Helen_Kent">Grace Helen Kent</a>&nbsp;and J. <li>1910 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Boris Sidis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boris_Sidis">Boris Sidis</a>&nbsp;opened the private Sidis Psychotherapeutic Institute at Maplewood Farms in Portsmouth, New Hampshire for the treatment of nervous patients using the latest scientific methods.</li>

<li>1953 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Alfred Kinsey" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Kinsey">Alfred Kinsey</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="mw-redirect" title="Sexual Behavior in the Human Female" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexual_Behavior_in_the_Human_Female">Sexual Behavior in the Human Female</a></em>.</li>

<li>1953 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="David McClelland" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_McClelland">David McClelland</a>&nbsp;proposed&nbsp;<a title="Need theory" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Need_theory">need theory</a>.</li>

<li>1953 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Harry Stack Sullivan" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_Stack_Sullivan">Harry Stack Sullivan</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em>The Interpersonal Theory of Psychiatry</em>, which holds that an individual's personality is formed by relationships.</li>

<li>1954 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="James Olds" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Olds">James Olds</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Peter Milner" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Milner">Peter Milner</a>&nbsp;of McGill University discovered the&nbsp;<a title="Reward system" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reward_system">brain reward system</a>, involving the brain's&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Pleasure center" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pleasure_center">pleasure center</a>.</li>

<li>1955 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Lee Cronbach" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_Cronbach">Lee Cronbach</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em>Construct Validity in Psychological Tests</em>, popularizing the concept of&nbsp;<a title="Construct validity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Construct_validity">construct validity</a>.</li>

<li>1959 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Lawrence Kohlberg" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawrence_Kohlberg">Lawrence Kohlberg</a>&nbsp;wrote his doctoral dissertation, outlining&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Kohlberg's stages of moral development" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kohlberg%27s_stages_of_moral_development">Kohlberg's stages of moral development</a>.</li>

<li>1994 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Michael Posner (psychologist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Posner_(psychologist)">Michael Posner</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Marcus Raichle" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcus_Raichle">Marcus Raichle</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em>Images of the Mind</em>, using&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Positron Emission Tomography" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron_Emission_Tomography">positron emission tomography (PET)</a>&nbsp;to localize brain cognitive functions.</li>

Smith</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em>A Dynamic Systems Approach to the Development of Cognition and Action</em>, a book on the use of developmental models based on dynamic systems.</li>
<li>1995 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Simon Baron-Cohen" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_Baron-Cohen">Simon Baron-Cohen</a>&nbsp;coined the term&nbsp;<a title="Mind-blindness" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mind-blindness">mental blindness</a>&nbsp;to reflect the inability of children with autism to properly represent the mental states of others.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>1996 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Giacomo Rizzolatti" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giacomo_Rizzolatti">Giacomo Rizzolatti</a>&nbsp;published his discovery of&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Mirror neurons" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror_neurons">mirror neurons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"></sup></li>

<h3><span id="2000s" class="mw-headline">2000s</span></h3>
<ul>
<li>2000 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Alan Baddeley" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_Baddeley">Alan Baddeley</a>&nbsp;updated his model of working memory from 1974 to include the&nbsp;<em>episodic buffer</em>&nbsp;as a third slave system alongside&nbsp;<em>the phonological loop</em>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<em>the visuo-spatial sketchpad</em><sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>2000 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Max Velmans" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Velmans">Max Velmans</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a title="Understanding Consciousness" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Understanding_Consciousness">Understanding Consciousness</a></em>, arguing for&nbsp;<a title="Reflexive monism" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflexive_monism">reflexive monism</a>.</li>
<li>2002 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Avshalom Caspi" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avshalom_Caspi">Avshalom Caspi</a>&nbsp;et al. presented a study that was the first to provide epidemiological evidence that a specific&nbsp;<a title="Genotype" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genotype">genotype</a>&nbsp;moderates children's sensitivity to environmental insults.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"></sup><sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>2002 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Steven Pinker" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steven_Pinker">Steven Pinker</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a title="The Blank Slate" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Blank_Slate">The Blank Slate: The Modern Denial of Human Nature</a></em>, arguing against tabula rasa models of the social sciences.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"></sup><sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>2002 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Daniel Kahneman" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Kahneman">Daniel Kahneman</a>&nbsp;won&nbsp;<a title="Nobel Prize" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize">Nobel Prize</a><sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>2007 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="George Mandler" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Mandler">George Mandler</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a class="new" title="A History of Modern Experimental Psychology (page does not exist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=A_History_of_Modern_Experimental_Psychology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1">A History of Modern Experimental Psychology</a></em><sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span id="2010s" class="mw-headline">2010s</span></h3>
<ul>
<li>2010 &ndash; The draft of&nbsp;<a title="DSM-5" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DSM-5">DSM-5</a>&nbsp;by the&nbsp;<a title="American Psychiatric Association" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Psychiatric_Association">American Psychiatric Association</a>&nbsp;(APA) was distributed for comment and critique.</li>
<li>2010 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Simon LeVay" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_LeVay">Simon LeVay</a>&nbsp;published&nbsp;<em><a title="Gay, Straight, and the Reason Why" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gay,_Straight,_and_the_Reason_Why">Gay, Straight, and the Reason Why</a></em>, which in 2012 received the Bullough Book Award for the most distinguished book written for the professional sexological community published in a given year.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>2012 &ndash; In 2009 America's professional association of endocrinologists established best practices for transgender children that included prescribing puberty-suppressing drugs to preteens followed by hormone therapy beginning at about age 16, and in 2012 the&nbsp;<a title="American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Academy_of_Child_and_Adolescent_Psychiatry">American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry</a>&nbsp;echoed these recommendations.<sup id="cite_ref-Transgender_at_10_89-0" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>2012 &ndash; The&nbsp;<a title="American Psychiatric Association" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Psychiatric_Association">American Psychiatric Association</a>&nbsp;issued official position statements supporting the care and civil rights of transgender and gender non-conforming individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>2013 &ndash; On 2 April U.S. President&nbsp;<a title="Barack Obama" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barack_Obama">Barack Obama</a>&nbsp;announced the 10-year&nbsp;<a title="BRAIN Initiative" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BRAIN_Initiative">BRAIN Initiative</a>&nbsp;to map the activity of every neuron in the human brain.</li>
<li>2013 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="DSM-5" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DSM-5">DSM-5</a>&nbsp;was published by the American Psychiatric Association (APA). Among other things, it eliminated the term "gender identity disorder," which was considered stigmatizing, instead referring to "<a title="Gender dysphoria" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gender_dysphoria">gender dysphoria</a>," which focuses attention only on those who feel distressed by their gender identity.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>2014 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Stanislas Dehaene" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanislas_Dehaene">Stanislas Dehaene</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Giacomo Rizzolatti" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giacomo_Rizzolatti">Giacomo Rizzolatti</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Trevor Robbins" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trevor_Robbins">Trevor Robbins</a>, were awarded the&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Grete Lundbeck European Brain Research Prize" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grete_Lundbeck_European_Brain_Research_Prize">Brain Prize</a>&nbsp;for their research on higher brain mechanisms underpinning literacy, numeracy, motivated behaviour, social cognition, and their disorders.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>2014 &ndash;&nbsp;<a title="Brenda Milner" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brenda_Milner">Brenda Milner</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Marcus Raichle" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcus_Raichle">Marcus Raichle</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="John O'Keefe (neuroscientist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_O%27Keefe_(neuroscientist)">John O'Keefe</a>&nbsp;received the&nbsp;<a title="Kavli Prize" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kavli_Prize">Kavli Prize</a>&nbsp;in Neuroscience for the discovery of specialized brain networks for memory and cognition<sup id="cite_ref-Kavli_93-0" class="reference"></sup></li>

<li>7 August 2015 &ndash; The&nbsp;<a title="American Psychological Association" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Psychological_Association">American Psychological Association</a>&nbsp;barred psychologists from participating in national security interrogations at sites violating international law.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"></sup></li>
<li>27 August 2015 &ndash; A team led by&nbsp;<a title="Brian Nosek" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brian_Nosek">Brian Nosek</a>&nbsp;of the&nbsp;<a title="University of Virginia" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Virginia">University of Virginia</a>&nbsp;published an article in&nbsp;<em>Science</em>&nbsp;that revealed that only 39 of 100 studies published in major psychology journals could be replicated.</li>
